# Transformation Rules
*Synthesised from general resume best-practice principles. Source of truth for all Stage 2 tailoring decisions.*

---

## Rule 1 — Resume Type Selection

Choose based on where the candidate is in their career and what needs to be foregrounded.

| Type | Best for | Section order |
|---|---|---|
| **Chronological** | Candidates with significant experience and continuous career progression | Brand Statement → Summary of Qualifications → Experience → Education → Skills |
| **Combination** | Early career with strong skills; career pivots where skills need to lead | Brand Statement → Skills → Experience → Education |
| **Functional** | Students, very limited experience, major career change | Brand Statement → Skills → Education → Experience |
| **Infographic** | Creative industries only (design, photography, publishing) | Varies — ATS-hostile, never for roles using ATS |

**Default: Chronological** for candidates with 7+ years of continuous experience.

**Critical:** In Chronological, Skills comes LAST — after Education. Experience is the primary evidence and leads. Placing Skills before Experience is the Functional order, which signals limited experience.

---

## Rule 2 — Header

Every resume begins with a header containing:
- **Name** — the largest, boldest element on the page. Make it unmissable.
- **Location** — city and country only. Never include a street address or unit number.
- **Contact** — phone number and email address.
- **Links** — LinkedIn URL; personal site or portfolio if it adds value for the specific role.

The header must appear on **every page**. On page 2 onwards, reduce the size — but keep it present. Hiring managers print resumes; pages get separated.

## Rule 3 — Personal Brand Statement

Use a **Personal Brand Statement**, not an Objective.

Objective statements are almost identical across candidates — everyone wants roughly the same things. A brand statement is unique because it captures what the candidate is passionate about and what makes their approach distinct.

**A brand statement answers three questions:**
1. What are you passionate about, or what problem do you love solving?
2. What is your unique approach — what do you do differently?
3. What is your ultimate goal?

**Construction rules:**
- 2–3 sentences maximum.
- Lead with the value proposition — not "I have X years of experience."
- Rewrite for each application — angle it toward what this specific role and company value.
- Avoid: "I am a hardworking professional...", "I am looking for a role where...", generic adjectives without proof (passionate, results-driven, dynamic).

---

## Rule 4 — Summary of Qualifications

Not required, but highly effective. Does the legwork of proving fit before the reader reaches the experience section.

**Construction rules:**
- 4–6 bullet points only.
- Each bullet = one qualification that directly maps to a requirement in this JD.
- **Mirror the JD's exact wording** where it genuinely applies — ATS systems scan for these phrases verbatim.
- The JD's qualifications list is a cheat sheet. Go through it, identify every qualification the candidate has, and reflect it back using their language.
- Adapt, don't copy-paste: expand or adjust to the candidate's actual context.
- Lead with the strongest credential for this specific role.
- Specific beats general: "15 years leading data teams in regulated financial environments" beats "extensive data experience."

## Rule 5 — Skills Section

Three styles — choose based on industry and role:

### 5a. Proven Skill Style
Best for: quota or goal-oriented roles (sales, operations, account management).
Format: **Skill name in bold** followed by a short description demonstrating it.
The bold allows a scanning reader to pick up skill names without reading the proof sentences.

### 5b. Creative Skills Style
Best for: creative industries (design, photography, publishing, visual arts).
Format: graphical representation of skill level — commonly a 5-star model.
Rule: if many skills would rate 3 stars or below, avoid this style. Low ratings actively work against the candidate.

### 5c. Subcategory Style
Best for: technical roles requiring multiple categories of skill (e.g., both technical and leadership).
Format: group skills by domain with a bold label for each group.
Example: `Technical: Python, SQL, Airflow` | `Leadership: Team management, Roadmap ownership` | `Strategy: Data architecture, AI adoption`

**Universal rule:** Specific beats general.
- Weak: "Communication"
- Strong: "Executive stakeholder communication — C-suite reporting, data governance advocacy"

Always check the JD for the exact skills listed and mirror the terminology.

## Rule 6 — Experience Section & Accomplishment Statements

### 6a. Core principle
Every bullet is an accomplishment statement — never a list of duties.

Capture: how you made an impact, how you improved something, how you changed something, how you got results. The key question is *how*.

**Banned openers:** "Responsible for", "Worked on", "Assisted with", "Helped", "Participated in", "Was involved in", "Managed" (without a result attached).

### 6b. The RAP Method
Break every accomplishment into three parts:

| Part | Question | Position in sentence |
|---|---|---|
| **Result** | What was the outcome or impact? | Lead with this |
| **Action** | What did you specifically do to achieve it? | Middle |
| **Problem** | What challenge were you solving? | Context / end |

**Example transformation:**
- Weak: "Processed customer orders in a timely fashion."
- Strong: "Increased orders processed by 10% per day by identifying common order types and batching — reducing bottlenecks in the fulfilment queue."

### 6c. Quantification rule
Numbers on a resume get disproportionate attention. Quantify wherever possible:
- Percentage increases or decreases
- Budget or cost figures
- Team size or headcount managed
- Number of users, clients, or systems affected
- Time saved
- Scale (locations, jurisdictions, integrations, pipelines)

Only quantify where genuine. If a result is not quantifiable, write the strongest honest qualitative statement. Never fabricate numbers.

### 6d. Strong action words
Begin every bullet with a strong action verb. See `action_words.md` for the full bank.

Quick reference by intent:
- Built something new → Architected, Engineered, Launched, Established, Designed
- Improved something → Streamlined, Accelerated, Reduced, Optimised, Overhauled
- Led people → Spearheaded, Directed, Mentored, Cultivated, Grew
- Analysed / found → Diagnosed, Identified, Forecasted, Mapped, Audited
- Delivered results → Generated, Boosted, Maximised, Recovered, Secured

"Managed" is acceptable only when immediately followed by team size and outcome:
- ✓ "Managed a team of 12 engineers, reducing pipeline failures by 40%"
- ✗ "Managed a data platform project" → replace with Piloted, Directed, Led

### 6e. Ordering experience entries
- Chronological: most recent first.
- Within each role: order bullets by impact — strongest first, not chronological.
- If a role has many bullets, cut to the 3–5 that best match this specific JD.

### 6f. Align bullets to the JD
Before finalising, return to the JD. If the employer is looking for specific experience the candidate has, ensure at least one bullet explicitly demonstrates it using their terminology.

### 6g. Visual scanning within bullets (extension beyond standard rules)
Bold the key skill or technology term *inside* the bullet text — not only the opening phrase. This lets a scanning reader spot important keywords without reading every word.
- Bold 1–3 terms per bullet maximum. Over-bolding removes the effect.
- The opening bold phrase (the result) should be scannable on its own as a headline.

Example:
- Weak: Built pipelines using Airflow and Python for 60+ stakeholders.
- Strong: **Designed and delivered 30+ ETL/ELT pipelines** (data quality, anomaly detection, reporting) using **Airflow** and **Python** — serving 60+ internal stakeholders across cross-functional teams.

## Rule 7 — Education Section

**Standard content:** school name, degree, programme, years attended, expected graduation date (if still enrolled).

**Expand where it adds value:**
- Academic achievements: GPA (if strong), honours, Dean's List, awards. Include qualifying criteria so the employer understands what it meant.
- Relevant courses: list courses directly relevant to the role, especially if the degree was in an adjacent field.

**Ordering:**
- Highest or most relevant education first.
- If continuing education or certifications are more relevant than the degree, list those first.
- For candidates with significant career experience whose highest qualification is secondary school: omit secondary school. It makes the resume appear more junior. Let skills and experience carry the weight instead.

## Rule 8 — Other Sections (Projects, Extracurriculars, Volunteer)

### Projects
Treat exactly like experience: use accomplishment statements in RAP format.
Include for candidates who have side projects or personal work that demonstrates skills relevant to the role.

### Extracurriculars and Clubs
Include only if they demonstrate skills or experience not already evident elsewhere.
Write accomplishment statements — membership alone is insufficient.
Most valuable for post-secondary graduates or candidates with limited work history.

### Volunteer Experience
Include only if: (a) recent, and (b) it reveals values or skills relevant to the employer.
Connect the dots explicitly — don't expect the employer to infer the relevance.
Exclude if the last volunteer experience was more than 5–7 years ago, or if including it means cutting more important sections.

As a general rule: include any section that makes the candidate a stronger match for this specific role. Exclude anything that doesn't directly support the case.

## Rule 9 — Length and Visual Hierarchy

### Length
- One page: infographic style, or candidates with minimal experience who cannot fill two pages.
- Two pages: candidates with enough experience, career history, and relevant projects. Do not exceed two pages.
- Page 1 gets more attention. Ensure the brand statement, summary, and most relevant experience are on page 1.
- Older or less relevant roles: reduce to 1–2 bullets each, or compress into an "Earlier career" block.

### Visual hierarchy
Choose 1–3 visual differentiation techniques and apply them consistently. Do not use all of them at once — the effect is lost.

Recommended set for most professional roles:
- **Size** — name largest, section headers medium, body text smallest
- **Weight** — bold for section headers and key terms
- **Colour** — one accent colour for headers; conservative for regulated industries (dark navy, charcoal)

Do not use: multiple fonts, italics throughout, underlines, multiple colours, text boxes (ATS-hostile), tables in body content (ATS-hostile), images or logos.

## Rule 10 — ATS Compliance

Applicant Tracking Systems scan resumes for keywords before a human sees them. To pass:

- Use standard section names: "Work Experience", "Education", "Skills", "Projects" — not creative alternatives.
- Avoid: text boxes, tables in body content, headers/footers containing key content, columns, images.
- Save as `.docx` for most applications — better ATS parsing than PDF. Use PDF only if specifically requested.
- Mirror JD keywords verbatim — but only where they genuinely apply to the candidate's experience.
- Do not keyword-stuff. ATS screening is followed by human review. Inaccurate keyword mirroring will surface in the interview.

---

## Rule 11 — Gap Handling

- Never leave unexplained date gaps on the resume.
- Every gap deserves a brief honest entry: sabbatical, caregiving, freelance, upskilling, personal project — all are legitimate.
- Reframe adjacent experience honestly. Do not fabricate. Gaps are far less damaging than dishonesty.
- A current gap (active job search) can be framed as: "Deliberate career transition — [what you're doing during the gap]."
